We Did Not Win a Darwin Award

Chapter 4 Vehicle Misadventures

“The difference between a car and a coffin is sometimes just good brakes and better judgment.” - Anonymous Traffic Safety Instructor

4.1 Introduction

Humans have been trying to kill themselves with vehicles since the invention of the wheel. We drive too fast, we drive too distracted, we drive under the influence of substances both legal and illegal, and we make split-second decisions that should absolutely result in our removal from the gene pool.

And yet, somehow, many of us survive.

This chapter collects stories from people who treated traffic laws as suggestions, physics as a negotiable concept, and common sense as optional equipment. They lived to tell their tales. Barely.

4.10 Reflections on Vehicles and Velocity

Vehicles give us incredible power—the power to travel at speeds our bodies were never designed to handle, to transport tons of cargo, to traverse vast distances. With that power comes responsibility, and too often, we forget that.

The survivors in this chapter all share similar realizations:

  1. Speed kills: What feels fast and fun can become deadly in seconds
  2. Distraction is deadly: Taking your eyes off the road for “just a second” is enough
  3. Vehicles are weapons: A car can kill as easily as a gun
  4. Physics always wins: You can’t brake your way out of certain situations
  5. Luck runs out: Every near-miss is borrowed time

We were all fortunate. We all had that moment where time slowed down, where we realized we’d made a terrible mistake, where we thought, “This is it.” And then, through some combination of luck, skill, and divine intervention, we walked away.

We didn’t win a Darwin Award that day.

But we came close enough to see it in the rearview mirror.
