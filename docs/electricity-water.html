We Did Not Win a Darwin Award

Chapter 3 Electricity and Water

“Water and electricity: The universe’s way of testing whether you paid attention in physics class.” - Murphy’s Law, Extended Edition

Somewhere in the collective unconscious of humanity, we all know that electricity and water don’t mix. We’re told this from childhood. It’s in every movie, every safety manual, every warning label. And yet, with remarkable consistency, people find creative ways to ignore this fundamental truth.

This chapter collects stories from those who learned—the hard way—that H₂O and high voltage are not friends.

The Backyard Pool Incident

Contributed by Marcus T., Phoenix, Arizona

I was twenty-three and had just bought my first house. It came with a pool, which I thought was the height of sophistication. What I didn’t have was money for a pool service, so I decided to maintain it myself. How hard could it be?

The filter pump had been making a weird noise, and after extensive research (by which I mean I watched one YouTube video), I determined it needed cleaning. The problem was that the pump was partially submerged in water from a recent rain that had flooded the equipment area.

“I’ll just unplug it,” I thought, wading into the ankle-deep water in flip-flops.

Here’s what I didn’t know: The pump was hardwired. There was no plug. The switch was inside the garage, which I hadn’t bothered to check.

I reached down to fiddle with what I thought was a connection point. The moment my hand touched the metal housing, every muscle in my body locked up. I couldn’t let go. I couldn’t scream. I couldn’t move. Time seemed to slow down as electricity coursed through me, and all I could think was, “This is the stupidest way to die.”

My neighbor, Miguel, who happened to be walking by, saw me through the fence. He didn’t touch me—smart man—but he ran to my garage and started flipping every breaker until one of them cut the power.

I collapsed into the water, gasping, my heart doing a drum solo. Miguel called 911. The paramedics checked me over, lectured me extensively, and confirmed I’d live. The ER doctor later told me I was incredibly lucky—the current could easily have stopped my heart.

The electrician I hired to properly fix everything (after I got out of the hospital) asked me what I’d been thinking. I didn’t have a good answer then, and I don’t have one now.

I no longer own a pool. I live in an apartment. I don’t even change light bulbs without triple-checking they’re off.

Lesson learned: Electricity doesn’t care about your YouTube education. Hire professionals.

Tales from the Bathroom

The Hair Dryer Chronicles

Contributed by Anonymous, Toronto, Canada

Look, I KNOW you’re not supposed to use a hair dryer near water. Everyone knows that. There are warning labels. There are PSAs. There was probably a very special episode of some TV show about it.

But I was running late, my hair was wet, and I’d just gotten into the bathtub to shave my legs because the lighting was better there. The hair dryer was on the counter, plugged in from that morning. I thought, “I’ll just reach over and grab it for a second. I’ll be careful.”

The cord caught on the shampoo bottle. The hair dryer tumbled toward the tub in slow motion, like something from an action movie. I watched it fall, still running, directly toward the water I was sitting in.

I moved faster than I’ve ever moved in my life. I launched myself backward, hit my head on the tile, and somehow managed to catch the dryer one-handed before it hit the water. I fumbled it, bobbled it, and finally gripped it with both hands, holding it above my head like Simba in The Lion King, my heart trying to escape my chest.

For about thirty seconds, I just sat there, dripping, holding the hair dryer, having a small existential crisis about my life choices.

Then I unplugged it, got out of the tub, sat on the floor, and cried a little.

I now dry my hair in the bedroom. The bathroom outlet has a GFCI. I have therapy appointments.

Lesson learned: Multitasking has limits. Those limits include electricity and bathtubs.

The Bathroom Radio

Contributed by James K., Liverpool, UK

In my defense, it was the ’90s, and we didn’t have phones to bring into the bathroom for entertainment. What we had was a small radio that sat on the counter, and I loved taking long baths while listening to the Sunday evening request show.

One evening, the radio reception got fuzzy. I reached over to adjust the antenna—which extended out over the tub—while I was still in the water. The radio’s position on the counter wasn’t stable, and it wobbled.

I tried to catch it.

I should not have tried to catch it.

My wet hand grabbed the radio just as it tipped into the water. For a fraction of a second, I felt the jolt start to run through me. Then—miracle of miracles—the plug popped out of the wall socket. The whole thing had been barely plugged in, and my jerking motion to catch the radio pulled it free.

I sat there in the tub with a dead radio in my hand, water sloshing everywhere, my life having flashed before my eyes.

The next day, I bought a waterproof Bluetooth speaker. Twenty-five years later, I still get anxious when electronics are anywhere near the bathroom.

Lesson learned: Bad reception isn’t worth dying for. Also, check your plugs.

The Hot Tub Electrician

Contributed by Dave R., Seattle, Washington

I work in IT, which means I’m legally required to believe I can fix anything electronic. This confidence does not always serve me well.

We had a hot tub in the backyard that kept tripping the breaker. I decided to troubleshoot it myself. I should mention that it was raining—classic Seattle weather—and the hot tub was full of water. Obviously.

I opened the electrical panel on the side of the tub. I saw a loose wire. I thought, “I’ll just tighten that connection real quick.”

Did I turn off the breaker first? Reader, I did not.

Did I consider that I was standing in a puddle while wearing wet shoes? Also no.

I reached in with a screwdriver—metal handle, naturally—and touched the live connection.

The shock threw me backward. I landed on my ass in the puddle, seeing stars. My arm felt like I’d punched a hornet’s nest from the inside. My wife came running out, took one look at me sprawled in the mud, and said, “Did you turn off the power first?”

“No,” I wheezed.

“Idiot,” she said, which was fair.

She called an actual electrician, who fixed the problem in ten minutes and charged us $150. He told me that if the current had gone across my chest instead of down my arm, I’d probably be dead.

I stick to IT now. If it doesn’t have a keyboard, I don’t touch it.

Lesson learned: Your degree in one field doesn’t grant you magical powers in another. Also, breakers exist for a reason.

The Aquarium Heater Incident

Contributed by Sarah L., Austin, Texas

I had a 75-gallon saltwater aquarium that was my pride and joy. The heater died one day, and my fish were getting cold. I ordered a replacement on Amazon, but it wouldn’t arrive for two days. I couldn’t let my expensive fish suffer.

So I had a brilliant idea: I’d use a space heater to warm the room. Then I had an even more brilliant idea: What if I just put the space heater close to the aquarium? Then I had my most brilliant idea yet: What if I suspended the space heater OVER the open-top aquarium to warm the water directly?

I rigged up this elaborate system with some wire and a chair. The space heater dangled about six inches above the water, blowing hot air down onto the surface.

I went to make coffee.

When I came back five minutes later, the wire had slipped. The space heater was now hanging at an angle, its bottom edge about two inches from the water’s surface. It was swaying slightly.

I watched in horror as it swung just a little too far and the corner dipped into the water.

There was a loud POP, a flash of light, and every fish in my tank simultaneously freaked out. The breaker tripped. The room went dark. I stood there in silence, contemplating my choices.

Miraculously, the fish all survived. The space heater did not. Neither did the breaker, which had to be replaced. The electrician asked me what happened, and I lied because I was too embarrassed to tell him the truth.

Lesson learned: Patience is a virtue. Amazon two-day shipping is faster than fish funeral planning.

Reflections on Voltage and Regret

The common thread in all these stories is simple: We knew better. Every single one of us had been taught that electricity and water are a dangerous combination. But in the moment—rushed, distracted, overconfident—we ignored what we knew.

Electricity is invisible, which makes it easy to underestimate. Water is familiar, which makes it easy to grow complacent around. Together, they’re a combination that can kill in seconds.

We’re lucky. We’re alive to tell these stories, to laugh nervously about them, to share them as cautionary tales. But luck runs out. Physics doesn’t negotiate.

If there’s one thing this chapter should teach you, it’s this: That voice in your head saying, “This is probably a bad idea?” That voice is correct. Listen to it.

And for the love of all that is holy, turn off the breaker first.

Key Takeaways:

  • Water + Electricity = Darwin Award nomination
  • GFCIs (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters) save lives—install them in bathrooms and near water
  • “I’ll just be careful” is not a safety plan
  • If you don’t know what you’re doing with electricity, hire someone who does
  • Wet hands, wet floors, and wet anything else should mean “do not touch electrical equipment”
  • Your overconfidence is not an adequate substitute for training
  • Breakers exist for a reason—use them

We survived our encounters with electricity and water, but the universe gave us a warning. We’re passing that warning on to you.

Don’t make our mistakes. Your fish, your hair, and your family will thank you.
