We Did Not Win a Darwin Award

Chapter 2 Gravity Lessons

“What goes up must come down. The question is whether you come down in one piece.” - Newton’s Lesser-Known Corollary

Gravity is perhaps the most consistent force in our lives. It never takes a day off. It never forgets. It never makes exceptions for stupidity or hubris. And yet, time and time again, human beings seem to forget that gravity is not just a good idea—it’s the law.

This chapter collects stories from people who tested gravity’s patience and somehow lived to regret it.

The Physics of Falling

Before we dive into these tales, let’s establish some facts:

  • Acceleration due to gravity: 9.8 m/s²
  • Terminal velocity for a human: ~120 mph (53 m/s) in a spread-eagle position
  • Survivable fall height: Highly variable, but anything over 20 feet is considered “serious”
  • Number of times gravity has lost: 0

With that cheerful information in mind, meet our survivors.

2.1 “The Roof Wasn’t As Close As It Looked”

Contributor: Jake M., California Age at incident: 19 Height of fall: ~25 feet Injuries: Fractured ankle, bruised ego, lifetime ban from apartment complex

I was a sophomore in college, and my buddies and I had decided that the best way to get to the roof of our apartment building for a party was not through the boring internal stairwell, but by climbing up the outside.

Yes, you read that right. We had roof access. Legal, safe, boring roof access via stairs. But where’s the fun in that?

The building was three stories, and there were decorative concrete ledges every floor. My friend Chad made it look easy—he practically scampered up like a drunk, overconfident squirrel. So naturally, I figured I could do the same.

The first two floors went fine. I was feeling invincible. I was feeling cool. I was feeling like Spider-Man, if Spider-Man had consumed six beers and had zero superpowers.

On the third ledge, about 25 feet up, I made a critical error. I looked down.

Now, looking down wouldn’t have been a problem if I hadn’t suddenly realized how incredibly stupid this was. My hands got sweaty. My confidence evaporated. And I did what any rational person would do in that situation—I panicked and tried to jump the last few feet to the roof.

I missed.

More specifically, I got my hands on the roof edge, but my feet found nothing but air. For a moment, I hung there, and I actually had time to think: “This is how I die. They’re going to put this in the campus newspaper. My mom is going to kill me. Wait, the fall will kill me first.”

Then my grip failed.

Here’s where I got lucky: I didn’t fall straight down. I bounced off the ledge I’d been standing on, which changed my angle. Instead of hitting the concrete patio below, I crashed through the branches of a medium-sized tree and landed in the decorative bark mulch of the landscaping.

The tree absolutely destroyed me—branches whipped my face, scraped my arms, and one particularly aggressive limb caught me right in the ribs. But it also slowed my fall enough that when I hit the ground, I only fractured my ankle instead of shattering every bone in my body.

I lay there in the mulch, staring up at the stars, listening to my friends screaming from the roof, and realized I’d just used up probably a decade’s worth of luck in about five seconds.

The resident advisor found me there. The paramedics came. My parents were called. I was banned from the apartment complex (though I was graduating that year anyway).

But I was alive.

Lesson learned: Stairs exist for a reason. Also, plant more trees—they save idiots like me.

2.2 “Parkour Is Not For Everyone”

Contributor: Anonymous, UK Age at incident: 22 Height of fall: ~12 feet Injuries: Concussion, shattered wrist, demolished pride

The year was 2009, and parkour was everywhere on YouTube. Every other video showed impossibly athletic people leaping between buildings, vaulting over railings, and generally making it look like human beings were meant to fly.

I watched these videos religiously. I was in decent shape. I’d done gymnastics as a kid. I thought, “How hard can it be?”

The answer: Very hard.

My first mistake was not starting small. My second mistake was choosing an urban location with concrete surfaces. My third mistake was doing this alone, without telling anyone where I was going.

I’d picked a spot between two commercial buildings where there was about a six-foot gap between rooftops, both about twelve feet high. The parkour videos made this look trivial—you just run and jump, land in a roll, done.

I climbed up to the first roof using a dumpster and a drainpipe (mistake number four). The rooftop was flat, graveled, and seemed solid enough. I backed up to get a running start, psyched myself up, and sprinted toward the edge.

Here’s what I didn’t account for: 1. Gravel provides terrible traction for a running start 2. The gap was actually closer to eight feet, not six 3. The far roof was slightly lower, changing the angle 4. I’d never actually practiced a running jump of any distance 5. Fear kicks in right at the edge

I launched myself into the air with all the grace of a drunk penguin. For a glorious half-second, I thought I might make it. I could see the other roof. I could see where I’d land. I could almost feel the triumph.

Then physics kicked in.

I didn’t make it. Not even close. My hands barely grasped the edge of the far roof, my body slammed into the brick wall, and I fell straight down into the alley below.

I landed on my left side, my arm outstretched to break the fall (which it did—by shattering my wrist). My head bounced off the concrete. The world went white, then dark, then swirly.

I don’t know how long I lay there. Could have been seconds, could have been minutes. When I came to, I was staring up at a narrow slice of sky between buildings, my arm was screaming at me, and my head felt like it was full of angry bees.

I tried to move. Bad idea. Everything hurt. My wrist was bent at an angle that wrists should not bend. I tasted blood. I might have vomited—the memory is fuzzy.

Eventually, I managed to pull out my phone with my good hand and call 999. The ambulance took forty minutes to find me because I couldn’t clearly explain where I was. The paramedics asked what happened, and I mumbled something about “fell off a wall.”

The diagnosis: Concussion, fractured wrist in three places, bruised ribs, various lacerations, and severely damaged pride. The hospital staff were professional enough not to laugh, but I saw the looks they exchanged.

Recovery took months. I had to have surgery on my wrist with pins and plates. I still can’t rotate it fully. And every time it rains, it aches—a permanent reminder of the day I thought I was Spider-Man.

Lessons learned: - YouTube skills are not transferable to real life - Professional parkour practitioners train for years and still get hurt - Always tell someone where you’re going - If you’re going to do something stupid, at least start small - Gravity doesn’t care about your confidence level

I did not win a Darwin Award, but I came remarkably close. The doctor said if I’d landed a few inches differently, I could have cracked my skull or broken my neck.

These days, I stick to the stairs.

Collected Short Tales: When Gravity Reminded Us

2.2.1 The Ladder Incident

Submitted by Robert T., Texas

“Ladders are only safe if you use them correctly. I learned this while cleaning gutters and decided to ‘just lean a little farther’ rather than climb down and move the ladder. Spoiler: I moved faster than the ladder did. Three broken ribs and a new respect for proper ladder safety protocols.”

2.2.2 The Hammock Failure

Submitted by Maria V., Oregon

“I installed a hammock between two trees in my backyard. What I didn’t account for was the weight rating, the quality of the rope, or the basic laws of physics. I climbed in with my girlfriend, a cooler of drinks, and my dog. The combined weight was approximately 350 pounds. The hammock was rated for 200. We fell six feet onto the ground. The dog landed on his feet. We did not.”

2.2.3 The Zipline Miscalculation

Submitted by Chris P., Colorado

“Built a backyard zipline from my deck to a tree. Used hardware store parts and YouTube instructions. Did not properly calculate the tension, the angle, or the stopping mechanism. First ride ended with me slamming into the tree at about 15 mph. Separated shoulder, black eye from a branch, and a wife who said ‘I told you so’ for the next six months.”

2.2.4 The Balcony Shortcut

Submitted by Tyler K., Florida

“Spring Break, hotel room on the third floor, locked myself out on the balcony. Brilliant idea: climb over to my buddy’s balcony next door. It was only about four feet across. I got halfway over, hanging from the railing, when I realized I couldn’t reach the other side. Hung there for what felt like forever before my arms gave out. Fell into the pool below by sheer luck. Could’ve easily been the pavement.”

2.2.5 The Tree Climbing Lesson

Submitted by Ashley M., North Carolina

“Age 30. Climbed a tree to rescue my cat. The cat came down on its own after ten minutes. I was stuck for an hour because I’d climbed higher than I realized and was suddenly terrified. Fire department had to come get me. The cat judged me from the ground. My neighbors still bring it up at parties.”

Reflections on Gravity

What is it about human nature that makes us continually underestimate gravity? Perhaps it’s our bipedal evolution—we spent so long learning to stand upright that we forgot how easy it is to fall down. Perhaps it’s the illusion of control—we feel like we’re choosing to be up high, so surely we can choose not to fall.

Or perhaps we’re just idiots sometimes.

Every person in this chapter survived their encounter with gravity, but not without cost. Broken bones, scars, medical bills, and the eternal knowledge that gravity was there, waiting patiently for us to screw up.

The survivors in this chapter share a common trait: they all had a moment of clarity mid-fall where they realized the magnitude of their error. And they all had just enough luck—a tree, a pile of snow, a fortuitously placed awning, a pool—to give them a second chance.

Gravity doesn’t usually give second chances.

They won’t make the same mistake twice. They’ve learned their lesson. They did not win a Darwin Award.

But it was closer than any of them would care to admit.

The Science of Falling

For those interested in the physics of why these stories exist:

Free fall acceleration: Objects fall at 9.8 m/s², meaning your speed increases by about 22 mph every second you’re falling.

Distance and velocity: - 10 feet: ~17 mph impact - 20 feet: ~25 mph impact - 30 feet: ~30 mph impact - 50 feet: ~39 mph impact

Survivability: Highly variable depending on: - Landing surface (water, grass, concrete, snow) - Body position on impact - Age and physical condition - Sheer dumb luck

Most survivable falls: People have survived falls from airplanes (with extraordinary circumstances). The record is over 30,000 feet.

Least survivable falls: Falls from as little as 6-10 feet can be fatal if you land wrong, especially headfirst onto concrete.

The lesson? Height matters, but how you fall and where you land matter just as much.
