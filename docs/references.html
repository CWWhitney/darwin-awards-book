About the Darwin Awards

The Darwin Awards have been documenting nature’s most unfortunate examples of natural selection since the 1990s. For those interested in learning more about the “original” awards:

Primary Sources

  • DarwinAwards.com - The official Darwin Awards website, maintained by Wendy Northcutt
  • Northcutt, Wendy. The Darwin Awards: Evolution in Action. Dutton, 2000.
  • Northcutt, Wendy. The Darwin Awards II: Unnatural Selection. Dutton, 2001.
  • Northcutt, Wendy. The Darwin Awards III: Survival of the Fittest. Dutton, 2003.
  • Northcutt, Wendy. The Darwin Awards 4: Intelligent Design. Dutton, 2006.

Academic Background

  • Darwin, Charles. On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection. John Murray, 1859.
  • Darwin, Charles. The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex. John Murray, 1871.

Safety Resources

While this book is intended as entertainment, we take safety seriously. If you’re inspired to NOT win a Darwin Award yourself, these resources can help:

General Safety

  • OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration): www.osha.gov
  • National Safety Council: www.nsc.org
  • Red Cross First Aid and Safety: www.redcross.org

Fire Safety

  • National Fire Protection Association: www.nfpa.org
  • U.S. Fire Administration: www.usfa.fema.gov
  • Emergency number: 911 (US), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), 112 (EU)

Electrical Safety

  • Electrical Safety Foundation International: www.esfi.org
  • Always hire licensed electricians for electrical work
  • Install GFCI outlets in bathrooms, kitchens, and outdoor areas

Wildlife Safety

  • National Park Service Wildlife Safety: www.nps.gov/subjects/wildlife/safety.htm
  • Bear Safety Center: www.bear.org
  • Leave No Trace: www.lnt.org
  • Remember: A fed bear is a dead bear. A stupid human is a Darwin Award winner.

Water Safety

  • American Red Cross Water Safety: www.redcross.org/take-a-class/swimming
  • U.S. Coast Guard Boating Safety: www.uscgboating.org
  • Never swim alone
  • Always wear life jackets on boats

General Outdoor Safety

  • REI Expert Advice: www.rei.com/learn/expert-advice
  • Mountaineers Books Safety Series: Various titles
  • Always tell someone where you’re going and when you’ll return

Medical Emergency Resources

If you find yourself in a situation that might qualify you for a Darwin Award:

  • Emergency Services: 911 (US), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), 112 (EU)
  • Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222 (US)
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741

Contributing to This Book

We’re always looking for more stories of people who narrowly avoided winning a Darwin Award. If you’d like to contribute your story:

Submission Guidelines

  1. You must have survived - This is non-negotiable
  2. The story must be true - We’re not collecting fiction (the truth is funny enough)
  3. You must own the rights - Don’t send us someone else’s story without permission
  4. Details can be changed - We respect anonymity and privacy
  5. Length: 500-3000 words preferred, but we’re flexible

What We’re Looking For

  • Close calls - Moments where you should have died but didn’t
  • Lessons learned - What did this experience teach you?
  • Honesty - Own your mistakes; we all make them
  • Humor - If you can laugh about it now, so can we
  • Universal themes - Stories that readers can relate to

What We’re NOT Looking For

  • Stories where others were seriously harmed
  • Deliberate criminal activity (accidental stupidity is fine)
  • Stories that encourage dangerous behavior
  • Anything that would constitute a how-to guide for harm

Legal Disclaimer

READ THIS PART:

  1. Do not attempt anything described in this book. These are cautionary tales, not instruction manuals.

  2. The stories are true to the best of our knowledge, but details have been changed to protect privacy. Names, locations, and identifying information may have been altered.

  3. We are not responsible if you read about someone touching a moose and think “I should try that.” That’s called a “Darwin Award waiting to happen.”

  4. This book is for entertainment purposes only. It is not a substitute for professional safety training, common sense, or basic survival instincts.

  5. If you’re reading this and thinking “That doesn’t sound so dangerous,” please put the book down and re-evaluate your life choices.

A Final Word

The line between a near-miss and a tragedy is often measured in inches, seconds, or sheer dumb luck. Every person in this book crossed that line and lived to tell the tale.

We hope these stories make you laugh. We hope they make you cringe. Most of all, we hope they make you think twice before doing something spectacularly stupid.

Because the difference between a good story and a Darwin Award is whether you survive to tell it.

Stay safe. Stay smart. Stay alive.

And remember: We did not win a Darwin Award.

Let’s keep it that way.
