# Contributing to "We Did Not Win a Darwin Award"

Thank you for considering sharing your story of survival and stupidity! This collection wouldn't exist without brave (and foolish) individuals willing to share their near-death experiences.

## What We're Looking For

We want true stories from people who:

- **Survived something they shouldn't have** - Near-death experiences caused by poor judgment
- **Learned from the experience** - Reflection on what went wrong and what you learned
- **Can tell it with humor** - If you can laugh about it now (even nervously), so can we
- **Are willing to share publicly** - With your name or a pseudonym, in a published book

## Story Categories

We're collecting stories in these categories (but we're open to others):

### Currently Seeking:
- 🔥 Fire and explosives incidents
- 🪂 Falls, jumps, and gravity-related mishaps  
- 🦌 Wildlife encounters gone wrong
- ⚡ Electricity and water combinations
- 🚗 Vehicle misadventures (cars, motorcycles, boats, etc.)
- 💧 Water hazards (drowning, diving, swimming)
- 🧪 Chemistry experiments and chemical exposure
- 🏗️ DIY and home improvement disasters
- 🌍 Travel and international incidents
- 🏥 Medical emergencies you caused yourself
- 👶 Youthful idiocy (teenage years especially)
- 🎢 Stunts and dares gone wrong

## What We DON'T Want

Please do NOT submit stories that:

- ❌ Ended with serious permanent harm to others
- ❌ Are purely about someone else's stupidity (must be your story)
- ❌ Are entirely fabricated (embellishment is fine; pure fiction is not)
- ❌ Glorify criminal activity without appropriate reflection
- ❌ Could serve as a "how-to" for dangerous activities
- ❌ Involve harm to animals
- ❌ Are mean-spirited or mocking of others

## Story Format Guidelines

### Length
- **Ideal**: 1,000 - 3,000 words
- **Minimum**: 500 words (for short takes)
- **Maximum**: 5,000 words (epic tales)

We're flexible! If your story needs more or less space, that's okay.

### Structure

A good story typically includes:

1. **Setup**: Where were you? What was the situation?
2. **The Decision**: What choice led to the near-disaster?
3. **The Incident**: What actually happened? (This should be vivid and detailed)
4. **The Moment**: When did you realize you'd messed up?
5. **The Outcome**: How did you survive?
6. **The Aftermath**: Injuries, consequences, reactions
7. **Lessons Learned**: What did this teach you?
8. **Reflection**: How has it changed you?

### Writing Tips

**Do:**
- ✅ Be honest about your stupidity
- ✅ Use vivid, sensory details
- ✅ Include dialogue if relevant
- ✅ Show the progression from "this seems fine" to "I'm going to die"
- ✅ Explain your thought process (or lack thereof)
- ✅ Acknowledge the role of luck
- ✅ Reflect on what you learned
- ✅ Use humor where appropriate

**Don't:**
- ❌ Minimize the danger (we want to know how close you came)
- ❌ Blame others if it was your fault
- ❌ Make yourself sound heroic (this is about survival despite stupidity)
- ❌ Include graphic medical details unnecessarily
- ❌ Encourage others to try similar things

### Example Opening

> "I was sixteen, which is already a risky age for decision-making. It was a hot summer day in rural Montana, the kind where you can see heat waves rising off the pavement and every teenage boy is looking for something—anything—to do.
>
> My friend Jake and I had been tasked with clearing brush from behind his dad's barn. This involved a lot of dried grass, tumbleweeds, and the accumulated plant matter of several neglectful years. Jake's dad, in his infinite wisdom (sarcasm noted in retrospect), told us we could 'just burn it' to make the job easier.
>
> He gave us matches. He did not, however, give us detailed instructions on the proper use of accelerants..."

## What Happens After Submission

1. **Acknowledgment**: We'll confirm we received your story (within 1 week)
2. **Review**: Editorial team reviews for fit and quality (2-4 weeks)
3. **Editing**: If accepted, we'll work with you on any edits (collaborative process)
4. **Consent**: You'll sign a contributor agreement (you retain copyright)
5. **Publication**: Your story appears in the book!

## Rights and Permissions

### What You Keep:

## Privacy and Anonymity

You have options:

### Real Name
- Your actual name appears with the story
- Good for: Professional storytellers, building platform, owning your narrative

### Pseudonym
- Choose a fake name
- Good for: Privacy, protecting professional reputation, embarrassing stories

### Anonymous
- Labeled as "Anonymous" or "Contributor prefers anonymity"
- Good for: Very embarrassing stories, legal concerns, maximum privacy

### Details Changed
- We can change names, locations, and identifying details
- The essential truth and stupidity remain
- Good for: Protecting others involved, legal concerns

## Tips for Success

### Most Likely to Be Accepted:
- Well-written, engaging narratives
- Genuine reflection and learning
- Universal themes (we've all done stupid things)
- Good balance of humor and honesty
- Clear cause-and-effect (your decision → near-death)

### Less Likely to Be Accepted:
- Poorly written or very brief submissions
- Stories that are just about bad luck (no agency)
- Mean-spirited or mocking tone
- No reflection or lesson learned
- Very similar to already-published stories

## Sample Submission Checklist

Before you submit, ask yourself:

- [ ] Is this story substantially true?
- [ ] Did I nearly die or get seriously injured?
- [ ] Was it primarily my own fault/decision?
- [ ] Can I tell it in an engaging way?
- [ ] Have I included reflection on what I learned?
- [ ] Is it appropriate for a general audience?
- [ ] Do I have permission from others involved (if they're identifiable)?
- [ ] Have I proofread for basic errors?
- [ ] Have I included my name/pseudonym choice?
- [ ] Have I provided contact information?

## Inspiration

If you're struggling to write your story, consider these prompts:

- What was the exact moment you realized you'd made a terrible mistake?
- What were you thinking right before the incident?
- How did time feel during the experience?
- What did you say/think immediately after?
- How did others react?
- What would you tell your past self?
- How has this changed your behavior?
- What's the first thing you remember after?
